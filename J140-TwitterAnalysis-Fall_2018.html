Project Introduction¶

Project Outline

  • CSULB J104 - Student Twitter Engagement
    • CSULB - J104 - Social Media Communication
    • Professor Kehoe
    • Assignment - Students tweet twice per week, and must include #CSULB104
  • Phase 1 - Research Scope
    • Scrape Tweets including #CSULB104
    • Examine student/tweet sentiment, tweet frequency, and patterns in time posted or the subject of the week
  • Role - Principle Researcher/Data Scientist

Project Goals

  • Objective - Analyze J104 tweets to establish baseline understanding of student tweet behavior and sentiment
  • Research Tools -
    • Twitter Scraping - Twython
    • Data Processing - pandas/numpy
    • Visualization - seaborn
    • Sentiment Analysis - vaderSentiment
  • Areas of Interest -
    • User/Tweet Sentiment - positivity or negativity of a tweet, or a user's general positive or negative sentiment throughout the course. Sentiment scores fall between 1 (Completely Positive) and -1 (Completely Negative), while neutral scores are between .05 and -.05.
    • Tweet Frequency - the number of times a user posts
    • Course Topics - weeks of study during the course, and the specific subjects students were tweeting about
    • Day/Time tweeted - time/day hour a tweet was posted

Tweet/User - Key Takeaways¶

    • Vast majority of users were positive.
    • Negative users were most likely to be extremely negative than slightly negative.
  • Tweet Breakdown
    • Most tweets were positive, with a majority of positive tweets being extremely positive.

Weekly Tweet Frequency

  • Tweet frequency generally decreased from week to week, with a few notable spikes in postage frequency.
  • Students are most engaged at the beginning of course -
    • The first four weeks of the course had the highest level of student engagement throughout the course.
    • Week 2 (n = 119, 9.2%), week 3 (n = 113, 8.7%) and week 4 (n = 111, 8.7%)
  • Students were least engaged during middle of the course, and when not required to post -
    • Student tweet frequency steadily declined in weeks 5-9 compared to weeks 2-4.
      • Week 5 (n = 93, 7.2%), week 6 (n = 87, 6.7%), week 7 (n = 88, 6.8%), week 8 (n = 79, 6.1%), week 9 (n = 85, 6.6%)
    • Students not required to post in week 1 (Class Introduction) or week 13 (Thanksgiving Break), and those weeks had the lowest levels of social media participation in the course.
      • Week 1 (n = 55, 4.2%), week 13 (n = 26, 2%)
  • Students appear to be highly responsive to tweet and assignment reminders - second highest tweet post frequency occurred after professor reminders
    • Professor Kehoe in week 10 reminded students that tweeting was a graded class assignment, and lack of participation will result in a lower overall class grade.
    • Professor Kehoe also reminded students one last time in week 15, before the twitter assignment ended and final exams began.
    • Week 10, week 11, and week 15 were among the most frequently tweeted weeks, after considering weeks 2, 3, and 4.
      • Week 10 (n = 100, %), week 11 (n = 102, %), and week 15 (n = 98, %).2

Course Week - Key Takeaways¶

  • Highest engagement at beginning of course, and when prompted by the professor's reminders.

    • First four weeks of the course contained highest tweet frequency of the course
    • Middle sections of the course contained the lowest weekly tweet counts in the course
    • Student engagement consistently fell after the first four weeks of class, and only returned to levels found in the first four weeks after reminders from the Professor

  • Students generally only post when required - substantial dropoff in tweet frequency observed when tweeting was not required during the class introductory period, and also holidays

  • Weekly sentiment was universally positive throughout the course, with the exception of the Fake News/Business Advertising subject

  • Class subjects teaching practical skills were most positively recieved of the course

  • Sentiment and popularity are not colinear with one another

    • Most popular weeks not the most positively tweeted weeks
    • Further research is needed – some negative users stopped tweeting midway through the course – cause higher density of positive tweets

Course Week - Actionable Items¶

  • Consider adjusting the order of certain subjects to maintain consistent levels of student engagement - spread out theory and practice lectures more evenly throughout the course

  • Automated weekly email reminders for students to write their tweets before class

  • Expand the number of lectures/class sessions teaching practical skills, and decrease the use of theory earlier in the course

Review Findings and Recommendations¶

Findings Summary¶

  • Users
    • Majority of users were positive
    • Concerning negative users –
      • Most negative users most were intensely negative
      • Negative users became substantially less engaged throughout course
  • Tweets
    • Positive tweets made up just over half of all tweets posted, while negative and neutral tweets were almost evenly split
    • Negative users decline in posts did not correspond with a wholesale decline of negative tweets
  • Week and Subject
    • Students most engaged at start of course and became less engaged over time
    • Tweet sentiment seems to correspond with subject – not tweet frequency per week
    • Students more positively recieved course subjects regarding skills and career development over those subjects related to teaching theory.
  • Hour/Time
    • Tuesday most frequently posted day
    • Most tweets posted between 8pm-6am

Study Limitations¶

  • Course being conducted in Spring 2019 – further data collected could change observations made here.
  • Exploratory research –2018 Fall semester – need further data to support or negate hypotheses
  • High density of positive users – mean scores could be skewed by lack of negative user tweets
  • Descriptive – no stat testing to more definitively measure relationships among variables

Recommendations and Future Research¶

  • User sentiment and frequency modeling

    • Collect spring 2019 student data – note continued or divergent trends
    • Incorporate student grades and other class data for modeling purposes

  • Survey Component

    • Psychographic and demographic predictors of student’s overall sentiment
    • Open ended questions – ascertain self-reported times most likely to tweet, and ask why those times

  • Engaging students

    • Consider changing subject order– elicit specific reactions from students
    • Weekly tweet reminders
    • Twitter Assignment changes-
      • Require tweeting at specific times
      • Tweet responses to questions related to class subjects
